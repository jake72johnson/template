CPG brands die from cash, not P&L. We pay the manufacturer 45 days before we collect from customers. DTC channel (immediate cash)

DTC sales clear in 1-2 days via Stripe/Shopify Payments. Zero working capital drag. This is why Year 1 CCC is only ~30 days — we're 100% DTC.

DSO = 0 days · DTC %: 100% Year 1

Retail channel (90-day collections)

Target and Buy Buy Baby pay on net-90 terms. Once retail launches, we ship inventory and wait 90 days to collect. This is the working capital trap that kills emerging brands.

DSO = 90 days · Retail %: 0% Year 1, 30% Year 5

How we fund the working capital gap

1. Inventory financing. Companies like Wayflyer, Settle, and Kickfurther provide non-dilutive working capital advances against POs (typical: 80% of PO value, 6-12 month repayment, ~12-15% APR). We expect to access this in Year 2 once we have 12 months of sales data.

2. Factoring retail receivables. Triumph, Crestmark, and Faire all factor wholesale invoices at 1-3% fee for 95% advance, paid when retailer pays. Compresses DSO from 90 days to 1-2 days.

3. Manufacturer payment terms. By Year 3, our 60K+ unit volume tier unlocks net-60 payment terms with our manufacturer (vs current net-45). Adds 15 days of free working capital.

4. Optional Series A. If retail expansion accelerates faster than projected, a $1-2M Series A in Year 3 funds the working capital build for Target/Walmart at scale without dilution risk to early investors.

Fiscal year & timeline

Year 1 = July 2026 - June 2027 (FY2027). Years labeled Y1-Y5 in the model align to this fiscal calendar. The fiscal year is anchored to the launch cycle so Y1 captures a complete operating year (presale → launch → first holiday → first spring), not a partial calendar year.

Revenue recognition: accrual basis. Presale dollars collected July-September 2026 are recorded as deferred revenue until product ships in October 2026. Cash collected and recognized revenue diverge during Q3 2026 (presale period) and reconcile in Q4 2026. See "Year 1 quarterly view" on the Forecast tab for the quarter-by-quarter breakdown.

Year 1 Landed Cost

$13.32

What this means

Total per-unit cost to get product from factory floor to our 3PL warehouse. Manufacturing ($8.50) + customs and duties ($2.32) + inbound freight ($2.10) + 3PL receiving ($0.40) = $13.32. This is what investors call the "landed cost" — what hits our balance sheet before we ship to a customer.

Why we believe this

$8.50 manufacturing cost is a documented quote from our manufacturer, predicated on a 30,000-unit annual commitment. Customs at 25% of declared value reflects current Section 301 tariff schedules for the relevant HTS code. Freight quoted by our forwarder (LCL ocean, port to West Coast 3PL). 3PL receiving rate confirmed in our 3PL agreement.

Subscription attach rate

18%

What this means

A meaningful share of orders include subscription attach at checkout (Parent Nutrition Coach annual at $239.88). Subscription attach raises both per-order revenue AND lifetime value materially. Industry data shows 15-25% subscription attach is achievable for premium kids CPG with on-brand premium content offering.

Why this matters

Subscription attach improves unit economics on both sides simultaneously. CAC per customer stays the same (one ad click, one customer acquired), but annual revenue per acquisition jumps materially with subscription. Modeled CAC of $45 per customer translates to dramatically better LTV/CAC when 15% of customers add the annual subscription. This is one of the few CPG mechanics that lifts both revenue per customer AND marketing efficiency at once.

Why we believe this

Premium subscription-attached DTC brands consistently see 15-25% subscription attach rate. Ritual reports 60%+ subscription rate on their core SKUs (most customers convert to subscribe). What this means

The combined "haircut" from gross revenue to net revenue. Includes physical returns (8.0%), shipping damage (1.5%), chargebacks and fraud (0.8%). This is what investors call "gross-to-net" — the difference between what we charge and what we actually keep.

Toys see higher returns than apparel because of gift-giving (mismatched recipient), age-appropriateness errors, and physical breakage during shipping. Industry data puts toy/kids DTC at 12-18% gross-to-net (Q1 spikes to 25%+ from gift returns). Our 14.0% reflects the elevated returns reality of premium DTC kids products. Outbound shipping & 3PL fulfillment

$11.50/order

What this means

The all-in cost to get a single order from our 3PL warehouse to a customer's door. Per-ORDER cost (not per-unit), which means a starter bundle order with 3 SKUs ships once for $11.50, not $34.50. This is critical for margin math because ~40% of orders include 2+ SKUs.

The breakdown

Carrier postage (UPS Ground / USPS Priority): $6.50–$8.00 per order. 3PL pick-and-pack fee: $3.50–$4.50 per order. Packaging materials (box, dunnage, tape, label): $0.50–$1.00 per order. Total Year 1: $11.50/order, dropping to $8.50/order by Year 5 via volume rate negotiation, regional 3PL distribution, and shift to USPS Cubic for smaller orders.

3PL fixed costs (separate line)

In addition to per-order shipping, we pay 3PL fixed monthly fees: warehouse minimums (~$500-1,500/month), inbound receiving fees (~$30/pallet, ~50 pallets/year), and storage by cubic foot ($0.50-1.50/cf/month, scaling with inventory volume). Modeled as $18K Year 1, scaling to $40K Year 5 as we grow inventory and SKU count.

Why we believe this

Per-order shipping cost is documented in our 3PL master service agreement and pricing matrices from comparable premium DTC kids brands (Lovevery's similarly-sized order profile averages $10-13 fully loaded). The $8.50 mature target reflects the volume tier we hit at ~50K orders/year (carrier rates step down at 30K, 50K, 100K orders).

"Why is shipping per-order, not per-unit?"

A bundle order containing 3 SKUs ships in one box. Real shipping cost is the carrier rate for the package weight + the 3PL pick fee per box (not per item picked). Modeling this per-unit (multiplying by SKU count) would overstate shipping costs by ~40% for bundle-heavy order mixes — which is exactly our profile. The model now correctly attributes one shipping fee per order, regardless of bundle composition.

Risk register

Top 5 risks · honestly stated

Every forecast carries risk. Here are the five that could most materially impact our Year 1-5 projection, what would trigger them, and how we'd respond. Acknowledging risk explicitly is what separates an operator from a hopeful founder.

1. Tariff escalation on China-sourced inventory

Trigger: Section 301 tariffs increase from 25% to 35-50% on toy HTS codes (active risk through 2026).

Mitigation: Manufacturer is exploring Vietnam & Mexico capacity for Y3 onward. Each 5% tariff hike adds ~$0.46 to landed cost, absorbed half via volume tier negotiation, half via $1-2 price increase. Currency hedging via FX forwards on Q4 production runs.

2. CAC inflation on Meta & TikTok

Trigger: iOS privacy changes, Apple ATT, or platform algorithm shifts push toy/kids CAC above $55 in Year 1 (currently $43 modeled).

Mitigation: Channel diversification baked into the plan — influencer (~$180K Y1) and PR ($40K) reduce dependence on paid social. Email/SMS list-build from Day 1 creates owned audience. Downside case toggle stress-tests +20% CAC inflation.

3. Working capital squeeze on retail expansion

Trigger: Target/Buy Buy Baby net-90 payment terms create $400-600K working capital gap when retail reaches 30% of revenue (Year 3-4).

Mitigation: Inventory financing (Wayflyer, Settle) accessible by Year 2 at 12-15% APR, non-dilutive. Receivables factoring (Triumph, Faire) compresses DSO from 90 to 1-2 days at 1-3% fee. Optional Series A in Year 3 if retail accelerates faster than projected.

4. Holiday concentration / seasonality risk

Trigger: ~50% of kids nutrition industry revenue concentrates in Oct-Dec. A weak Q4 can wipe out the year. Macro recession or supply chain disruption ahead of Q4 is the worst case.

Mitigation: Q4 inventory locked in by Aug (8-12 weeks pre-season). Multi-channel distribution (DTC + wholesale + Amazon by Y3) reduces single-channel risk. Year-round marketing investments build evergreen demand for non-Q4 months.

5. Single-manufacturer concentration

Trigger: Quality issue, capacity shortage, factory shutdown, or pricing dispute with our single manufacturer.

Mitigation: Identified two backup manufacturers; sample runs by end of Y1 for redundancy. Quality control via third-party inspection (Asia Inspection / SGS) on every PO. CPSIA compliance and ISO 8124 testing built into manufacturer SOW.

How this forecast was built

1. Bottom-up unit economics first. We started by validating that a single unit can be sold profitably (AOV − landed cost − fulfillment − CAC = positive contribution). It can: $28.99 contribution per unit at our base case.

2. Year 1 volume anchored to manufacturing commitment. 30,000 units committed, 20,000 modeled as Y1 sell-through. The remaining 10K rolls into Y2 at zero additional production cost.

3. Years 2–5 driven by industry-benchmarked growth curves. We don't assume hockey-stick growth. Our curve decelerates from 85% → 50% → 30% → 18%, which matches what successful DTC CPG brands actually do.

4. Operating leverage modeled explicitly. Marketing as a percentage of revenue drops from ~72% (Y1) to ~25% (Y5) as the brand pulls. Fixed costs (labor, G&A) grow slower than revenue.

5. Conservative where data is thin, aggressive where data is strong. Returns at high end of industry. Manufacturing cost based on signed quote. CAC at high end of toy/kids range. AOV mid-market.

Why category leaders command a premium

The market values brand strength differently from product strength. Same revenue, different multiple. Same EBITDA, different exit. Here is the framework.

A commodity DTC brand at $50M revenue with 15% EBITDA margins exits for roughly $150M. A category-defining DTC brand at the same $50M revenue exits for $250-400M. The reason is not the product. It is that category leaders have pricing power, lower CAC, higher retention, and acquisition optionality that commodity brands do not.

The investor math: a commodity brand has to keep spending to maintain customer acquisition. A category leader has organic demand, word-of-mouth flywheels, and strategic acquirer interest. The multiple jumps from 2-3x to 5-8x as category leadership becomes credible.

The path goes through three gates: prove product-market fit and unit economics (Y1-Y2), build the brand into a category synonym (Y3-Y4), and become the obvious strategic acquisition target (Y5+). Most brands never make it past Gate 1. Category leaders make it to Gate 3.

The honest warnings

No model is complete without acknowledging what could go wrong. The dream is real, but so are the gates that block it.

Survivorship bias

For every Olipop there are 200 DTC brands that never scaled past $5M. Most never make it past Gate 1. The category looks easy from outside because we only see the winners. Plan for the floor, not the ceiling.

Strategic acquirers find you

You do not pitch Unilever or P&G. They find you when your category position is so strong that buying you is cheaper than building competing capability. Path C requires earned attention, not requested attention.

Time horizon

Category leadership takes 5-10 years. Brand equity does not compound on a quarterly cadence. Path C is a decade of compounding, not a 3-year sprint. Plan capital and patience accordingly.

The anchoring truth

Path A is the floor. Path B is the realistic stretch. Path C is the upside we build toward.

Every decision in Y1 should keep all three paths viable. Don't optimize for Path A and lose the upside. Don't bet everything on Path C and lose the floor. Build the brand, build the customers, build the data. Then the acquirers come.
